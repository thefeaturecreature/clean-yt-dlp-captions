## TL;DR

Matz discusses Ruby’s future, including gradual type-checking tools (like JetBrains’ runtime type info) and experimental concurrency models like *Guild* to address race conditions. He shares his time split (20% coding, 60% admin/engagement), highlights the core team’s decentralized structure (100+ ‘committees’ with varying roles), and admits Ruby’s concurrency model—inherited from Perl—needs modernization. On legacy decisions, he regrets early thread implementation but praises Ruby’s block concept. Ruby 3 aims for minimal breaking changes, focusing on gradual evolution (e.g., keyword args, frozen string literals). He also touches on community bridges, M Ruby’s stability, and his rare public recognition.
